Analytic Strategy

üîç My Chosen Method: Reflexive Thematic Analysis
‚ñº

I used Braun & Clarke's reflexive thematic analysis approach to analyze focus group data from secondary and post-secondary educators discussing college readiness. This method allowed me to move systematically from raw data to meaningful patterns while remaining reflexively aware of how my own positionality shaped interpretation.

The analysis process involved:

1. Data familiarization through repeated reading and initial note-taking
2. Systematic coding using both descriptive (literal) and analytical (interpretive) codes
3. Theme development by clustering codes into candidate patterns
4. Theme refinement through iterative review against the full dataset
5. Final interpretation that addresses research questions holistically

This approach aligns with my social constructivist framework, acknowledging that meaning-making is collaborative and value-laden rather than objective or neutral.

The Analysis Process

üîÑ My Iterative Coding Journey
‚ñº

I began the analytic process by conducting multiple close readings of the interview and focus group transcripts to familiarize myself with both the content and the emotional tone of the data.

During this phase, I generated a set of descriptive codes to capture what participants explicitly stated. Examples include soft skills, self-advocacy, parental involvement, and lack of a clear definition. These codes anchored the literal and surface-level meanings in the data.

As I continued engaging with the transcripts, I developed analytical codes, which allowed me to interpret the deeper structures and patterns embedded within participants' comments. These included codes such as rigor gaps, misalignment between systems, failure avoidance, communication breakdowns, and siloed systems. Analytical coding required me to move beyond what was said to interpret what these statements suggested about the broader educational ecosystem.

With both descriptive and analytical codes in place, I began clustering related codes together. I printed my code list, spread out excerpts, and created visual maps to experiment with multiple ways of grouping the meanings. I asked myself: What patterns are emerging? How do these codes interact? What story are they collectively telling about college readiness?

Several clusters began to form naturally:

I then re-examined the dataset to ensure each cluster was grounded in the data rather than my assumptions. I checked for internal coherence within clusters and distinctiveness between them. Some early clusters were collapsed, and others were refined to better reflect the central organizing concepts emerging from participant dialogue.

Interpretation

A holistic meaning-making across the themes and the full dataset

üìñ The Holistic Portrait
‚ñº

The data from both the individual interview and the focus group reveal a complex and layered portrait of college readiness that extends far beyond academic preparation. When examined holistically, the themes expose shared concerns, diverging perspectives, systemic silences, and underlying power dynamics that shape how readiness is constructed, perceived, and acted upon in K‚Äì12 and higher education contexts.

üåü Commonalities Across Participants ‚ñº

Across all participants, there was a strong consensus that traditional indicators of readiness‚Äîgrades, test scores, and curriculum benchmarks‚Äîdo not accurately reflect a student's preparedness for college or adult life. Participants described readiness in terms of soft skills, independence, executive functioning, resilience, and communication. The repeated emphasis on self-advocacy and the ability to navigate bureaucratic systems suggests that readiness is as much about navigating institutions as it is about academic content.

Another shared pattern was the belief that systems intended to support students are insufficiently coordinated. Participants across both data sources described working toward similar goals‚Äîpreparing students for postsecondary success‚Äîbut doing so in isolation, with mismatched expectations and inadequate communication. This sense of fragmentation appeared in discussions about LMS systems, transition planning, parental access, grading policies, and external agencies.

Finally, the majority of participants expressed a desire for cross-sector collaboration. Participants recognized that the readiness problem is too complex and too systemic for any one sector to address alone, and they viewed structured opportunities for collaborative dialogue as both necessary and overdue.

‚öñÔ∏è Divergences Between Perspectives ‚ñº

While commonalities were strong, several key divergences emerged:

1. Diverging Understandings of Responsibility

Participants disagreed‚Äîsometimes subtly, sometimes overtly‚Äîon who is responsible for readiness development.

K‚Äì12 educators tended to point toward systemic constraints, inequitable rigor, grading pressures, and parental expectations.

Postsecondary experiences (as reflected in Kima's commentary) emphasized skill deficits and misaligned expectations.

This divergence reveals a tension: readiness is framed simultaneously as an instructional issue, a behavioral issue, and a structural issue, depending on one's position within the system.

2. Diverging Experiences With Rigor

Participants from different school contexts described drastically different levels of academic rigor, reinforcing the theme that readiness varies widely based on the school a student attends.

This divergence was particularly pronounced in the focus group, where participants described inequities even between schools only minutes apart.

3. Diverging Views on Standardized Testing

While all participants acknowledged testing limitations, the intensity of criticism varied.

Some saw tests as mildly unhelpful; others saw them as actively harmful‚Äîobscuring real readiness indicators, privileging memorization over critical thinking, and creating false narratives about student capability.

üîá Silences in the Data ‚ñº

Silences‚Äîwhat participants did not say‚Äîare equally revealing.

1. The Absence of Student Voice

While participants frequently discussed student preparedness, no students were included in the conversation. Their true experiences, motivations, fears, and hopes remain unspoken, and thus the data reflect adult interpretations rather than student perspectives.

This silence reflects a broader systemic tendency for educators to speak about students rather than with them.

2. Racial and Socioeconomic Dynamics

Although participants touched on inequitable access and systemic burdens for families, they did not explicitly discuss how race, class, or linguistic diversity intersect with readiness.

Given well-established disparities in educational opportunity, this silence suggests possible discomfort or lack of awareness in naming structural inequities explicitly.

3. Institutional Incentives

There was no discussion of how grading policies, standardized testing requirements, or resource allocations are shaped by district, state, or federal policies.

This silence may reflect the extent to which educators feel these forces are immutable or beyond their control.

üèõÔ∏è Power and Systemic Structures ‚ñº

The themes reveal multiple forms of systemic power that shape readiness:

1. Institutional Power

Standardized tests, grading systems, and LMS structures were portrayed as unchallenged authorities that dictate student success, despite their misalignment with real readiness indicators.

These systems hold tremendous power over both teaching and student identity.

2. Professional Power Hierarchies

K‚Äì12 and higher education actors occupy different positions in the educational hierarchy.

K‚Äì12 educators described constraints, parent pressures, and high accountability; higher ed expectations assume student independence without consideration of prior supports.

These structural hierarchies reinforce the very misalignment participants identified.

3. Family Power Dynamics

Parents hold significant influence‚Äîsometimes supportive, often overwhelming or obstructive.

Their access to information and comfort navigating systems directly affects student transitions, amplifying inequities for families with fewer resources or less familiarity with educational institutions.

Reflexivity

How my positionality, assumptions, and lived experience shaped interpretation‚Äîand how the data shaped me

ü™û Entering the Research: My Starting Point
‚ñº

As a Black woman, a former English teacher, and now an instructional designer working at the intersection of K‚Äì12 and higher education, I entered this research with a deeply personal and professional connection to the problem of college readiness.

My assumptions were shaped by years of observing students arrive in my college classes underprepared for the type of thinking, writing, and self-direction expected of them. I also carried assumptions about systemic fragmentation‚Äîthe belief that secondary and postsecondary systems operate in silos, rarely communicating in meaningful ways.

Throughout the analysis, I became increasingly aware of how these assumptions both clarified and complicated my interpretation of the data.

üåü What Challenged My Preconceived Notions ‚ñº

One of the most surprising aspects of the data was the extent to which K‚Äì12 educators described readiness not through academic lenses but through behavioral and emotional competencies. I expected soft skills to emerge, but I underestimated how central they were to participants' definitions‚Äîso central that academic indicators became secondary or even peripheral.

This challenged my earlier belief that readiness failures were primarily academic or curricular. Instead, educators highlighted resilience, communication, executive functioning, and the ability to persist through difficulty as the foundational skills students were missing.

This shift required me to interrogate my original focus on curriculum alignment. I realized I had, perhaps unconsciously, privileged the academic side of readiness because of my discipline-specific background in English. The data forced me to widen my lens and acknowledge the extent to which readiness is fundamentally relational, behavioral, and developmental.

‚úÖ What Aligned With My Expectations ‚ñº

The theme of systemic misalignment‚Äîsiloed systems, inconsistent rigor, communication gaps‚Äîaligned strongly with my initial assumptions. Hearing Monica describe the lack of cross-sector communication and Victoria describe the dramatic differences in rigor across districts reaffirmed my belief that readiness is hindered by structural fragmentation rather than teacher-level shortcomings.

Additionally, the critique of standardized tests mirrored my long-standing skepticism of large-scale assessments as indicators of capability. Participants' frustration reinforced what I had previously observed: test results can mask deep inequities and often fail to capture the competencies that matter most for student success.

‚ö° Moments Where My Subjectivity Was Activated ‚ñº

My identity as a Black woman and instructor to first-generation college students resurfaced strongly when participants spoke about equity and access barriers. For example, when Monica described how educational systems are burdensome and inaccessible for many families, I felt an immediate emotional resonance.

I recognized the systemic patterns she referenced‚Äîinstitutions whose structures privilege families who already know how to navigate them. This moment activated my own lived understanding of how education can marginalize certain communities, and I had to pause to ensure that I was not projecting my personal experiences onto participants' words.

Similarly, discussions about parental involvement stirred memories of my mother preparing me to enter spaces where others might underestimate me. This emotional undercurrent made me more attuned to the power dynamics embedded in parental access, institutional navigation, and student agency. Recognizing this allowed me to interpret the data with greater empathy‚Äîbut it also required deliberate reflexive attention to avoid over-identification with the experiences described.

üí´ Data That Evoked Emotional Responses ‚ñº

Some narratives, particularly those involving students who shut down when facing adversity, elicited strong feelings. I have witnessed this pattern repeatedly in my own classrooms, and the familiarity of these stories reminded me of the frustration and compassion I felt toward students who lacked support, guidance, or confidence.

These feelings heightened my awareness of how grading systems and instructional structures can unintentionally reinforce inequity. To maintain analytic clarity, I documented these emotions during coding to distinguish between what the data actually revealed and the affective responses it triggered.

üîç How My Positionality Shaped Interpretation ‚ñº

My positionality influenced my analysis in several ways:

1. Sensitivity to Systemic Inequities

Because of my lived experience, I naturally attended to structural barriers‚Äîunequal rigor, inaccessible systems, inconsistent parental resources. This heightened awareness allowed me to see how equity and access cut across nearly every theme.

At the same time, reflexivity required me to ensure that equity themes emerged organically from the data rather than from my prior commitments to social justice frameworks.

2. Awareness of Cross-System Misalignment

Working in both K‚Äì12 and higher ed made me particularly attuned to misalignment between the two sectors. This positionality made those patterns more visible to me‚Äîbut it also required me to guard against overstating misalignment where it was not explicitly described.

3. Recognition of Soft Skills as Essential

My experience with struggling first-year writers primed me to see soft skill deficits as academic barriers. The data expanded this view, revealing soft skills not simply as writing challenges but as survival skills necessary for navigating institutions and adulthood.

  • Intentional cultivation of soft skills
  • Acknowledgement of systemic inequities that shape students' transitions

It also reaffirmed my commitment to designing a collaborative workshop grounded in design thinking‚Äîone that invites educators to collectively redefine readiness, examine misalignment, and co-develop more equitable pathways.
